# How does Basil use worship as theological evidence?

## Question Type
Methodological / argumentative

## Why This QUD Is Central
This is the core methodological question of the dissertation. Basil's entire strategy in *On the Holy Spirit* depends on treating doxological practice as more than a stylistic matter. The question is what *kind* of evidence worship is for him, and how it functions within the argument.

## The Question Precisely Stated
Does Basil treat doxological practice as:
- **(A) Independent evidence** — worship implies divinity; the Spirit is worshipped; therefore the Spirit is divine
- **(B) Confessional confirmation** — worship expresses and safeguards what is already established by Scripture and the Spirit's operations
- **(C) A convergent argument** — neither alone is decisive, but worship + Scripture + operations together make the subordinationist position collectively incoherent

## Candidate Answers

### Answer A — Worship as Independent Proof
Basil sometimes argues as if the Church's *practice* of glorifying the Spirit is itself theologically dispositive. Strongest form: one cannot worship *through* an instrument without committing to that instrument as divine. The Church's practice therefore entails the Spirit's divinity whether opponents admit it or not.

**Evidence for A**: Phases 1 and 5 of *On the Holy Spirit*; the *agrapha* argument (ch. 27) gives unwritten ecclesial practice a measure of independent standing.

### Answer B — Worship as Confessional Confirmation
On this reading, doxology confirms and seals what Scripture and operations establish. Basil's scriptural survey (Phase 3) and the Spirit's operations (Phase 4) do the primary evidential work; the doxological defense in chs. 28–30 is the confessional conclusion, not the starting premise.

**Evidence for B**: The treatise's structure — Basil builds the scriptural and operational case before mounting the full doxological defense.

### Answer C — Convergent Argument
The most likely actual structure: no single strand is decisive, but the convergence of Scripture, baptism, operations, and doxology makes the subordinationist position incoherent as a total position.

**Evidence for C**: Basil's cumulative strategy; he does not claim doxology alone settles the question.

## Provisional Judgment
Answer C is probably closest to the text. But the dissertation must determine whether specific passages support A or B before concluding. **Do not flatten into a single answer.**

## Open Questions
- Does Basil ever state the argument in the explicit form: "we worship the Spirit, *therefore* the Spirit is divine"? Locate specific passages in the PDF.
- What is the relationship between the *agrapha* argument and the scriptural argument — parallel or hierarchical?
- How does the incoherence argument (you cannot worship *through* a creature) function — is it scriptural, logical, or both?
- Does Basil think worship can be wrong or misleading? If not, why not?
