# Contribution Claim

## Current Working Claim
This dissertation argues that selected patristic sources show doctrine, worship, and human transformation to be mutually interpretive, such that Basil’s pneumatological defense of worship and Maximus’s Christological anthropology together provide a richer account of divine agency and human becoming.

## Short Version
Patristic theology shows that worship is not secondary to doctrine and that divine agency is central to human transformation.

## Basil-Specific Contribution (now documentable)
Basil's argument in *On the Holy Spirit* is structurally convergent-confessional. His defense of the *syn* formula is not a claim from liturgical authority but a cumulative demonstration that the Pneumatomachian position is incoherent as a total account of Christian Scripture, worship, and salvation. Worship is the site where this incoherence becomes most practically unavoidable — not independent proof of the Spirit's divinity, not merely expressive ornament, but the place where the coherence of the entire argument is exposed.

This claim is more precise than the standard reading of Basil as simply "arguing from worship" and more honest about the limits of his doxological argument than readings that treat it as equivalent to a full *homoousios* assertion.
