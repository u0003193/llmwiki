# Matthew 28:19

## Text Boundaries
This note centers on the triadic baptismal formula in Matthew 28:19, while recognizing that the formula belongs within the broader commission context.

## Structure
- command to make disciples
- command to baptize
- naming of Father, Son, and Holy Spirit

## Observations
- the triadic naming formula is integral to the action of baptism
- the Spirit appears within the Church’s central initiatory confession
- the verse is especially important for Basil because it resists attempts to isolate the Spirit from the Father and the Son in ecclesial practice
- **Basil's "one name" argument**: Jesus commands baptism "into the *name*" (singular) of Father, Son, and Spirit — not three separate names. Basil reads this as indicating a unity of nature underlying the three: the Spirit is named within the one divine name, not alongside it as a lesser entity.
- this argument is in addition to the simple triadic listing; it presses the grammar of Matt 28:19 against the opponents' ranking scheme

## Textual / Translation Issues
No major issue is foregrounded here for the present argument. The major issue is not textual instability but theological use.

## QUD (Question Under Discussion)

### Explicit Question
What is the Church commanded to do in baptism?

### Implicit Question
How are Father, Son, and Holy Spirit named together in the Church’s saving rite?

### Answer
The Church is commanded to baptize in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit.

### Grounds
The triadic naming belongs to the command itself and is not peripheral to it.

### Implication
The Church's initiatory confession already places the Spirit within its fundamental sacramental and doctrinal life, making attempts to diminish the Spirit more difficult to sustain.

### Confidence
strong implicit

## Function in Argument
Supports Basil's claim that the Holy Spirit belongs integrally within the Church's confession and baptismal life rather than standing outside or below the Father and the Son. Basil explicitly addresses objections about baptism and insists that invocation of Christ cannot be severed from the fullness of Father-Son-Spirit confession.

**Opponent's counter**: even if the Spirit is named alongside Father and Son, naming does not entail ontological equality. Angels, prophets, and Moses appear in proximity to divine acts without implying coequality. The triadic formula could reflect proper liturgical ordering rather than identity of rank.

**Basil's reply**: the singular *name* (not three names) forecloses the ranking interpretation; moreover, the Spirit is not named as a witness or instrument of the act but as a co-agent in whom baptism is administered.

## Interpretive Options
- Option A: the triadic naming formula names the Spirit without implying equal dignity
- Option B: the triadic naming formula has confessional force and therefore materially supports Basil’s anti-subordinationist case

## Opposing Views
A strong opposing position says that even if the Spirit is named with Father and Son, ordering or naming alone does not settle ontological equality.

## Provisional Section Thesis
Matthew 28:19 gives Basil a scriptural-confessional anchor for resisting efforts to detach the Spirit from the Church’s baptismal and doctrinal life.
