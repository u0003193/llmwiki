## Main Thesis
Basil defends the full divine dignity of the Holy Spirit by coordinating Scripture, ecclesial worship, and theological reasoning.

## Key Claims — Focused on Research Question
- Doxological language is theologically significant; the dispute over *syn* vs *dia/en* is a doctrinal dispute, not a stylistic one.
- The Spirit's role in baptism (Matt 28:19) and salvation (Rom 8) resists reduction to creaturely instrumentality.
- Opponents misuse prepositions (*ek*, *dia*, *en*) to imply an ontological hierarchy Scripture does not support.
- The incoherence argument: one cannot worship *through* the Spirit while refusing to *glorify* the Spirit.
- The *agrapha* argument: ecclesial formulas not explicit in Scripture can still carry apostolic and theological weight.

## My Evaluation
- **strong**: integrated Scripture-worship-doctrine argument; the convergence of three modes (scriptural, liturgical, operational) gives cumulative force
- **weak**: opponent reconstruction depends entirely on Basil's polemical presentation; actual opponent texts not preserved
- **unclear / dissertation question**: whether worship is *independent evidence* or *confessional confirmation* of the Spirit's divinity — must not be resolved prematurely
- **gap**: Basil does not call the Spirit *homoousios*; this limits the explicit terminological force of his argument even as the substantive argument implies it
