# Worship and Doctrine

## Definition
The relationship between worship (especially liturgical and doxological practices) and doctrinal confession. In Basil of Caesarea, worship is not merely expressive but materially tied to what the Church believes about God.

## Key Language / Terms
- doxology
- glorification
- confession
- baptismal formula
- ecclesial practice

## Evidence

### Biblical
- [[Matthew 28-19]] — triadic confession embedded in baptism
- [[Romans 8]] — Spirit’s role in life and adoption

### Patristic
- [[Patristics/Basil - On the Holy Spirit]] — dispute over doxology shows worship is doctrinally significant

## Interpretive Options
- **Option A**: worship expresses doctrine but does not contribute to it — doctrine is determined by Scripture alone, worship follows
- **Option B**: worship manifests doctrine in lived form — the Church's doxological practice is itself a theological act, and altering it is a doctrinal act
- **Option C**: worship can function as convergent evidence when linked to Scripture and the Spirit's operations — neither alone is decisive

**The difference between B and C is the dissertation-level question for this research project.**

## Opposing Views
Worship is symbolic and rhetorical only; doctrinal claims must be derived independently from Scripture or philosophical reasoning. On this view, Basil's opponents were correct to insist that liturgical formulas do not settle metaphysical questions — what matters is whether the Spirit's rank can be established from Scripture, not from doxological practice.

## The Incoherence Argument (Basil)
Basil's strongest move: the opponents cannot coherently worship *through* the Spirit while refusing to *glorify* the Spirit. If the Spirit is the medium through whom divine worship ascends, then the Spirit must be the kind of being through whom divine worship can properly flow. To worship through a creature would be to direct worship through something that stands outside God — incoherent with the Church's soteriological claims about the Spirit.

This argument does not merely appeal to practice; it shows that the opponents' own practice (worshipping through the Spirit) already commits them to more than they admit.

## My Judgment
In Basil, worship is best understood as confessional theology—where doctrine is embodied and safeguarded rather than merely restated.

## Grounds
- Basil’s treatise arises from liturgical dispute, not abstract speculation
- he treats wording of praise as theologically significant
- he integrates worship with Scripture and salvation

## Implications
- theology must take ecclesial practice seriously
- doctrine and liturgy cannot be cleanly separated
- pneumatology affects actual Church life, not only conceptual systems

## Tensions / Open Questions
- How much *independent* evidential force does worship have in Basil — as opposed to confirming Scripture?
- Can worship mislead? Basil does not address this possibility, which leaves a potential gap in his argument.
- Does Basil's approach imply something like *lex orandi, lex credendi*? Caution: that formula is post-Basilian (Prosper of Aquitaine, 5th c.); applying it anachronistically can distort the reading.
